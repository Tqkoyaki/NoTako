Modeling

As previously stated, exponential functions and logarithmic functions are used to model countless read-world phenomena. These functions are invaluable to science and engineering as we are able to model and manipulate the models to make predictions and solve problems.

Exponential Growth and Decay​

For many exponential models, we use the general form y=A0ekty = A_0e^{kt}y=A0​ekt where A0A_0A0​ is equal to the value at time zero, eee is Euler's constant, and kkk is a constant that determines the rate of growth or decay. If the value of kkk is positive, the model represents exponential growth. If the value of kkk is negative, the model represents exponential decay.

The function y=A0ekty = A_0e^{kt}y=A0​ekt is a general function with the following characteristics:

Half-Life​

One common application of exponential decay is the concept of half-life which is the length of time it takes an exponentially decaying quantity to decrease to half of its initial value. Radiocarbon Dating​

Radiocarbon dating is a method used to calculate the approximate date a plant or animal died. This is possible through carbon-14 which is a radioactive isotope of carbon that has a half-life of 5,7305,7305,730 years. It occurs in small quantities in the carbon dioxide we breathe. Most of the carbon on Earth is carbon-12, which has an atomic weight of 121212 and is not radioactive. Scientists have determined the ratio of carbon-14 to carbon-12 in the atmosphere and use this information to determine the age of organic materials.

As long as a plant or animal is alive, the ratio of the two isotopes of carbon in its body is close to the ratio in the atmosphere. When it dies, the carbon-14 in its body decays and is not replaced. By comparing the ratio of carbon-14 to carbon-12 in a decaying sample to the known ratio in the atmosphere, the date the plant or animal died can be approximated.

Since the half-life of carbon-14 is 5,7305,7305,730 years, the formula for the amount of carbon-14 remaining after ttt years is A≈A0e(−ln⁡(2)/5730)tA \approx A_0e^{(-\ln(2) / 5730)t}A≈A0​e(−ln(2)/5730)t or A≈A0e−0.000121tA \approx A_0e^{-0.000121t}A≈A0​e−0.000121t. The decay rate of carbon-14 was determined using the formula k=−ln⁡(2)tk = \frac{-\ln(2)}{t}k=t−ln(2)​.

From the equation, A≈A0e−0.000121tA \approx A_0e^{-0.000121t}A≈A0​e−0.000121t, we know the ratio of the percentage of carbon-14 in the object we are dating to the initial amount of carbon-14 in the object when it was formed is r=AA0=e−0.000121tr = \frac{A}{A_0} = e^{-0.000121t}r=A0​A​=e−0.000121t. We solve this equation for ttt, to get t=−ln⁡(r)0.000121t = \frac{-\ln(r)}{0.000121}t=0.000121−ln(r)​ which is the formula used to calculate the age of the object.

Doubling Time​

The opposite of half-life is doubling time which is the time it takes for an exponentially growing quantity to double in value. Unlike, half-life and radiocarbon dating, the value of kkk is positive for doubling time because we have exponential growth.

To find the doubling time of a quantity, we can set y=2A0y = 2A_0y=2A0​ in the general exponential model to get the equation 2A0=A0ekt2A_0 = A_0e^{kt}2A0​=A0​ekt. We can solve for t=ln⁡(2)kt = \frac{\ln(2)}{k}t=kln(2)​ to find the doubling time of the substance. We could also find the growth constant kkk through k=ln⁡(2)tk = \frac{\ln(2)}{t}k=tln(2)​ by rearranging the formula.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

According to Moore's Law, the doubling time for the number of transistors that can be put onto a computer chip is approximately 222 years. Give a function that describes this behavior.

The function that describes the behavior is y=A0eln⁡(2)2ty = A_0e^{\frac{\ln(2)}{2}t}y=A0​e2ln(2)​t.

Newton's Law of Cooling​

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate at which an object cools is proportional to the difference in temperature between the object and the object's surroundings. This means the temperature of an object will exponentially approach the temperature of its surroundings and it will level off as it gets closer to the surrounding temperature.

Unless the room temperature is zero, this will correspond to a vertical shift of the generic exponential function. The temperature of an object, TTT, in surrounding air with temperature TsT_sTs​ will behave according to the formula T(t)=Aekt+TsT(t) = Ae^{kt} + T_sT(t)=Aekt+Ts​ where ttt is time, AAA is the difference between the initial temperature of the object and the surroundings, and kkk is a constant that determines the continuous rate of cooling of the object.

Given a set of conditions, we can apply the Newton's Law of Cooling by...

  1. Setting TsT_sTs​ equal to the y-coordinate of the horizontal asymptote (the surrounding temperature or ambient temperature).
  2. Substituting the given values into the continuous growth formula T(t)=Aekt+TsT(t) = Ae^{kt} + T_sT(t)=Aekt+Ts​ to find the parameters AAA and kkk.
  3. Substitute in the desired time to find the temperature of the object at that time.

Logistic Growth​

Exponential growth typically cannot continue indefinetly and so while exponential models may be useful in the short term, they tend to fall apart the longer they continue. Eventually, an exponential model must begin to approach some limiting value forcing the growth to slow down.

To achieve this, we use a logistic growth model which is approximately exponential at first, but it has a reduced rate of growth as the output approaches the model's upper bound, which is called the carrying capacity. The logistic growth model is f(t)=c1+ae−btf(t) = \frac{c}{1 + ae^{-bt}}f(t)=1+ae−btc​ where c1+a\frac{c}{1 + a}1+ac​ is the initial value, ccc is the carrying capacity or limiting value, and bbb is a constant determined by the rate of growth.

Models in Natural Base​

Powers and logarithms of any base can be used in modeling, but the two most common bases are 101010 and eee. It is important to note that in science and mathematics, the natural base eee is often preffered and so if we have a model in a different base, we typically convert it to the natural base.

Given a model with the form y=abxy = ab^xy=abx, we can change it to the form y=A0ekxy = A_0e^{kx}y=A0​ekx by...

Choosing Appropriate Models​

When choosing models to represent data, we often use three kinds of functions: linear, exponential, and logarithmic. If the data lies on a straight line or seems to lie approximately along a straight line, a linear model may be best. If the data is non-linear, we often consider an exponential or logarithmic model. It must be noted that other models, such as quadratic, may also be considered.

When choosing between an exponential and logarithmic model, we look at the concavity of the data which is the way the data curves. If we draw a line between two data points, and most of the data between those two points lies above that line, we say the curve is concave down. The shape of a concave down curve is similar to a bowl that bends downwards and therefore cannot hold water. On the other hand, if most of the data between two points lies below the line, we say the curve is concave up. The shape of a concave up curve is similar to a bowl that bends upwards and therefore can hold water.

In most common cases, an exponential curve is always concave up, and a logarithmic curve is always concave down. Finally, a logistic curve changes concavity. It starts out concave yp and then changes to concave down beyond a certain point, called a point of inflection.
