Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation is an equation containing a second-degree polynomial. An example of a quadratic equation is x2+3x+1=0x^2 + 3x + 1 = 0x2+3x+1=0. Finally note, ax2+bx+c=0ax^2 + bx + c = 0ax2+bx+c=0 where aaa, bbb, and ccc are real numbers, and if a≠0a \neq 0a=0, means the equation is in standard form.

Square Root Property​

We are able to solve quadratic equations using the square root property if there is no xxx term present and we can isolate the x2x^2x2. With the x2x^2x2 term isolated, the square root property states that if x2=kx^2 = kx2=k, then x=±kx = \pm\sqrt{k}x=±k​ where kkk is a nonzero real number.

4x2+1=74x^2 + 1 = 74x2+1=7

4x2=64x^2 = 64x2=6

x2=32x^2 = \frac{3}{2}x2=23​

x=±32x = \pm\sqrt{\frac{3}{2}}x=±23​​

Solving By Factoring​

We can solve most quadratic equations through factoring due to the zero-product property which states that if a×b=0a \times b = 0a×b=0, then a=0a = 0a=0 and/or b=0b = 0b=0, where aaa and bbb are real numbers or algebraic expressions.

If we factor a quadratic equation which is in standard form, we will get a product of two or more expressions that equal 0. We can set each individual expression in the multiplication to 0 to get a resulting value of xxx which is our solution.

x2−5x−6=0x^2 - 5x - 6 = 0x2−5x−6=0

x2−6x+x−6=0x^2 - 6x + x - 6 = 0x2−6x+x−6=0

x(x−6)+1(x−6)=0x(x - 6) + 1(x - 6) = 0x(x−6)+1(x−6)=0

Completing the Square​

Not all equations can be factored or solved using the square root property. For cases like this, we can use the method of completing the square where we add or subtract terms on both sides of the equation in order to obtain a perfect square trinomial.

The steps to solving using completing the square include...

  1. If a≠1a \neq 1a=1, divide the entire equation by aaa.
  2. Seperate the terms with variables on one side of the equation and the constant term on the other side of the equation.
  3. Multiply bbb by 12\frac{1}{2}21​ and square it. This results in (12b)2(\frac{1}{2}b)^2(21​b)2.
  4. Add (12b)2(\frac{1}{2}b)^2(21​b)2 on both sides of the equation. This gives us a perfect square trinomial on one side of the equation.
  5. Factor the perfect square trinomial into a perfect square.
  6. Use the square root property to solve the equation.

Discriminant​

The discriminant is the expression under the radical in the quadratic formula: b2−4acb^2 - 4acb2−4ac which tells us the nature of the solutions for any quadratic equation in standard form. It tells how many solutions to expect as well as telling us if the solutions are real numbers or complex numbers.

Value of DiscriminantInformation
b2−4ac=0b^2 - 4ac = 0b2−4ac=0One rational solution
b2−4ac>0b^2 - 4ac > 0b2−4ac>0, perfect squareTwo rational solutions
b2−4ac>0b^2 - 4ac > 0b2−4ac>0, not a perfect squareTwo irrational solutions
b2−4ac<0b^2 - 4ac < 0b2−4ac<0Two complex solutions (No real solution)

Pythagorean Theorem​

The pythagorean theorem is based on a right triangle and states the relationship among the lengths of the sides. The relationship of the sides is a2+b2=c2a^2 + b^2 = c^2a2+b2=c2 where aaa and bbb refer to the legs of a right triangle adjacent to 90°90\degree90°, and ccc refers to the hypotenuse.
