Law of Cosines

Even though the Law of Sines enables us to solve many oblique triangles, it does not help with triangles where the known angle is between two known sides or when all three sides are known but no angles are known. We refer to these triangles are SAS (side-angle-side) and SSS (side-side-side) triangles respectively and for these cases, we can use the Law of Cosines to find the unknown angles or sides.

Law of Cosines​

In order to derive the Law of Cosines, we need to use the Generalized Pythagorean Theorem, which is an extension of the Pythagorean Theorem for non-right triangles. To derive this theorem, we place an arbitrary non-right triangle ABCABCABC somewhere in the coordinate plane where the vertex AAA is at the origin, the side ccc is drawn along the xxx-axis, and the vertex CCC is located at some point (x,y)(x, y)(x,y) in the plane.

note

The triangle could have been placed anywhere in the plane but for the sake of simplicity, we placed it in this specific location making it easier to derive the theorem with.

When we draw a perpendicular line from CCC to the xxx-axis, we get a right triangle which lets us get these identities:

cos⁡θ=x (adjacent)b (hypotenuse)sin⁡θ=y (opposite)b (hypotenuse)x=bcos⁡θy=bsin⁡θ\begin{array}{cccccccccccccc} \cos \theta = \dfrac{x \text{ (adjacent)}}{b \text{ (hypotenuse)}} &&&& \sin \theta = \dfrac{y \text{ (opposite)}}{b \text{ (hypotenuse)}} \\[1.5em] x = b \cos \theta &&&& y = b \sin \theta \end{array}cosθ=b (hypotenuse)x (adjacent)​x=bcosθ​​​​sinθ=b (hypotenuse)y (opposite)​y=bsinθ​

Now if we use the side (x−c)(x - c)(x−c) as one leg of a right triangle and yyy as the second leg, we can find the length of hypotenuse aaa using the Pythagorean Theorem...

a2=(x−c)2+y2=(bcos⁡θ−c)2+(bsin⁡θ)2=(b2cos⁡2θ−2bccos⁡θ+c2)+(b2sin⁡2θ)=b2(cos⁡2θ+sin⁡2θ)+c2−2bccos⁡θa2=b2+c2−2bccos⁡θ\begin{array}{cccccccccccccc} a^2 &=& (x - c)^2 + y^2 \\[1em] &=& (b \cos \theta - c)^2 + (b \sin \theta)^2 \\[1em] &=& (b^2 \cos^2 \theta - 2bc \cos \theta + c^2) + (b^2 \sin^2 \theta) \\[1em] &=& b^2 (\cos^2 \theta + \sin^2 \theta) + c^2 - 2bc \cos \theta \\[1em] a^2 &=& b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos \theta \end{array}a2a2​=====​(x−c)2+y2(bcosθ−c)2+(bsinθ)2(b2cos2θ−2bccosθ+c2)+(b2sin2θ)b2(cos2θ+sin2θ)+c2−2bccosθb2+c2−2bccosθ​

The formula we derived is one of the three equations of the Law of Cosines and the other equations can be found in a similar fashion. However, all of these equations state that the square of any side of a triangle is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides minus the product of the other two sides and the cosine of the included angle. This means all the equations for the Law of Cosines are...

  1. a2=b2+c2−2bccos⁡αa^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos \alphaa2=b2+c2−2bccosα
  2. b2=a2+c2−2accos⁡βb^2 = a^2 + c^2 - 2ac \cos \betab2=a2+c2−2accosβ
  3. c2=a2+b2−2abcos⁡γc^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos \gammac2=a2+b2−2abcosγ

...where α\alphaα, β\betaβ, and γ\gammaγ are the angles opposite to sides aaa, bbb, and ccc respectively.

note

To make solving for an angle easier, we can rearrange the Law of Cosines in terms of cosine which gives us...

  1. cos⁡α=b2+c2−a22bc\cos \alpha = \dfrac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} \\[1.5em]cosα=2bcb2+c2−a2​
  2. cos⁡β=a2+c2−b22ac\cos \beta = \dfrac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2ac} \\[1.5em]cosβ=2aca2+c2−b2​
  3. cos⁡γ=a2+b2−c22ab\cos \gamma = \dfrac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}cosγ=2aba2+b2−c2​

Heron's Formula​

We can use the Law of Cosines to derive the Heron's Formula which is a valuable formula because it allows us to find the area of an oblique triangle when we know the lengths of all three sides.

To derive this formula, let aaa, bbb, and ccc be the sides of the triangle and α\alphaα, β\betaβ, and γ\gammaγ be the opposite angles to the sides respectively. To derive the formula, we can use any of the Law of Cosines equations to express one of the angles in terms of the sides. In this case, we can use the formula...

cos⁡γ=a2+b2−c22ab\cos \gamma = \dfrac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}cosγ=2aba2+b2−c2​

With this formula, aaa would be the base of the triangle meaning the altitude/height would be bsin⁡γb \sin \gammabsinγ. Finally, we will also need the trigonometric identity sin⁡γ=1−cos⁡2γ\sin \gamma = \sqrt{1 - \cos^2 \gamma}sinγ=1−cos2γ​. Putting this all together, we can derive Heron's Formula...

\cos^2 \gamma} \\[1.5em] &=& \dfrac{1}{2}ab \sqrt{1 - \left(\dfrac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab}\right)^2} \\[1.5em] &=& \dfrac{1}{2}ab \sqrt{1 - \dfrac{\left(a^2 + b^2 - c^2\right)^2}{4a^2b^2}} \\[1.5em] &=& \dfrac{1}{2}ab \sqrt{\dfrac{4a^2b^2 - Real World Applications​

Just like with the Law of Sines, the Law of Cosine is an invaluable tool in various fields because with both these laws, we can solve all possible oblique triangles.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

Suppose a boat leaves port, travels 101010 miles, turns 202020 degrees, and travels another 888 miles. How far from port is the boat?

We can label the values of the triangle as a=8a = 8a=8, b=10b = 10b=10, and γ=180∘−20∘=160∘\gamma = 180^\circ - 20^\circ = 160^\circγ=180∘−20∘=160∘. To find the distance from the boat to the port, we will need to find the value of the side ccc which we can do using the Law of Cosines...

c2=a2+b2−2abcos⁡γ=(8)2+(10)2−2(8)(10)cos⁡(160∘)=64+100−160cos⁡(160∘)=164−160cos⁡(160∘)c2≈314.35c≈314.35c≈17.7 miles\begin{array}{cccccccccccccc} c^2 &=& a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos \gamma \\[1.5em] &=& (8)^2 + (10)^2 - 2(8)(10) \cos(160^\circ) \\[1.5em] &=& 64 + 100 - 160 \cos(160^\circ) \\[1.5em] &=& 164 - 160 \cos(160^\circ) \\[1.5em] c^2 &\approx& 314.35 \\[1.5em] c &\approx& \sqrt{314.35} \\[1.5em] c &\approx& 17.7 \text{ miles} \end{array}c2c2cc​====≈≈≈​a2+b2−2abcosγ(8)2+(10)2−2(8)(10)cos(160∘)64+100−160cos(160∘)164−160cos(160∘)314.35314.35​17.7 miles​
