Polar Coordinates

The rectangular coordinate system where we have the xxx-axis and yyy-axis is an extremely useful tool in mathematics and engineering. However, sometimes there are applications that are circular in nature, and in such cases, polar coordinates can be more convenient.

Plotting Polar Coordinates​

The polar coordinate system is a two-dimensional system where the grid is represented as a series of concentric circles radiating out from the pole (the origin).

Each polar coordinate in this system is labeled as (r,θ)(r, \theta)(r,θ), where rrr is the distance from the pole and θ\thetaθ is the angle measured from the positive xxx-axis.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

Plot the point (3,π2)(3, \dfrac{\pi}{2})(3,2π​) on the polar grid.

The point is 333 units away from the pole because r=3r = 3r=3 and the angle is θ=π2\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2}θ=2π​ which means we are sweeping in a counterclockwise direction 90∘90^\circ90∘ from the polar axis. Putting this all together gives us the solution...

Transforming Between Coordinate System​

We often may need to convert between rectangular and polar coordinates which we are able to do using some basic relationships.

Off this diagram, we can see the following right triangles relationships which will help us in our transformations...

  1. cos⁡θ=xr⇒x=rcos⁡θ\cos \theta = \dfrac{x}{r} \Rightarrow x = r \cos \theta \\[1em]cosθ=rx​⇒x=rcosθ
  2. sin⁡θ=yr⇒y=rsin⁡θ\sin \theta = \dfrac{y}{r} \Rightarrow y = r \sin \theta \\[1em]sinθ=ry​⇒y=rsinθ
  3. r2=x2+y2r^2 = x^2 + y^2 \\[1em]r2=x2+y2
  4. tan⁡θ=yx\tan \theta = \dfrac{y}{x} \\[1em]tanθ=xy​

Firstly, we have two equations x=rcos⁡θx = r \cos \thetax=rcosθ and y=rsin⁡θy = r \sin \thetay=rsinθ which are equations that give us rectangular coordinates and are written in terms of polar coordinates. Therefore, these equations are used to convert from polar to rectangular coordinates through substitution.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

Write the polar coordinates (−1,2π3)(-1, \dfrac{2\pi}{3})(−1,32π​) in rectangular form.

From the polar coordinates, we know that r=−1r = -1r=−1 and θ=2π3\theta = \dfrac{2\pi}{3}θ=32π​. Using the equations above, we can find xxx and yyy as follows...

x=rcos⁡θy=rsin⁡θx=−1cos⁡2π3y=−1sin⁡2π3x=−1(−12)y=−1(32)x=12y=−32\begin{array}{cccccccccccccccccccc} x &=& r \cos \theta &&&& y &=& r \sin \theta \\[1em] x &=& -1 \cos \dfrac{2\pi}{3} &&&& y &=& -1 \sin \dfrac{2\pi}{3} \\[1em] x &=& -1 \left(-\dfrac{1}{2}\right) &&&& y &=& -1 \left(\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) \\[1em] x &=& \dfrac{1}{2} &&&& y &=& -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \\[1em] \end{array}xxxx​====​rcosθ−1cos32π​−1(−21​)21​​​​​yyyy​====​rsinθ−1sin32π​−1(23​​)−23​​​

Therefore, the rectangular coordinates are (12,−32)\left(\dfrac{1}{2}, -\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right)(21​,−23​​).

Instead of converting from polar to rectangular, we may need to convert from rectangular to polar. For this, we could use r2=x2+y2r^2 = x^2 + y^2r2=x2+y2 and tan⁡θ=yx\tan \theta = \dfrac{y}{x}tanθ=xy​ as both equations are written in terms of rectangular coordinates and can be used to find polar coordinates.

Write the rectangular coordinates (3,3)(3, 3)(3,3) in polar form.

From the rectangular coordinates, we know that x=3x = 3x=3 and y=3y = 3y=3. Using the equations above, we can find rrr and θ\thetaθ as follows...

r2=x2+y2tan⁡θ=yxr2=32+32tan⁡θ=33r2=9+9tan⁡θ=1r2=18θ=tan⁡−1(1)r=18θ=π4,5π4r=32θ=π4\begin{array}{cccccccccccccccccccc} r^2 &=& x^2 + y^2 &&&& \tan \theta &=& \dfrac{y}{x} \\[1em] r^2 &=& 3^2 + 3^2 &&&& \tan \theta &=& \dfrac{3}{3} \\[1em] r^2 &=& 9 + 9 &&&& \tan \theta &=& 1 \\[1em] r^2 &=& 18 &&&& \theta &=& \tan^{-1}(1) \\[1em] r &=& \sqrt{18} &&&& \theta &=& \dfrac{\pi}{4}, \dfrac{5\pi}{4} \\[1em] r &=& 3\sqrt{2} &&&& \theta &=& \dfrac{\pi}{4} \\[1em] \end{array}r2r2r2r2rr​======​x2+y232+329+91818​32​​​​​tanθtanθtanθθθθ​======​xy​33​1tan−1(1)4π​,45π​4π​​

So, the polar coordinates are (32,π4)\left(3\sqrt{2}, \dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)(32​,4π​).

Transforming Between Equations​

Just like with coordinates, we can convert equations between the polar and rectangular forms using all the trigonmetric relationships we have established. However, even though we can convert between the two forms in a similar manner, the process is significantly more difficult.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

Rewrite the Cartesian equation y2=6y−x2y^2 = 6y - x^2y2=6y−x2 in polar form.

The terms y2y^2y2 and x2x^2x2 make it difficult to directly substitute using x=rcos⁡θx = r \cos \thetax=rcosθ and y=rsin⁡θy = r \sin \thetay=rsinθ. However, we can use the relationship r2=x2+y2r^2 = x^2 + y^2r2=x2+y2 to help us instead. If we rearrange the equation, we can get the value of rrr...

y2=6y−x2x2+y2=6yr2=6rsin⁡θr2−6rsin⁡θ=0r(r−6sin⁡θ)=0r=0r−6sin⁡θ=0r=0r=6sin⁡θ\begin{array}{cccccccccccccccccccc} y^2 &=& 6y - x^2 \\[1em] x^2 + y^2 &=& 6y \\[1em] r^2 &=& 6r \sin \theta \\[1em] r^2 - 6r \sin \theta &=& 0 \\[1em] r (r - 6 \sin \theta) &=& 0 \\[1em] r = 0 && r - 6 \sin \theta = 0 \\[1em] r = 0 && r = 6 \sin \theta \\[1em] \end{array}y2x2+y2r2r2−6rsinθr(r−6sinθ)r=0r=0​=====​6y−x26y6rsinθ00r−6sinθ=0r=6sinθ​

We reject r=0r = 0r=0 as it is the trivial solution due to it only representing one point, (0,0)(0,0)(0,0).

Therefore, the polar form of the equation is r=6sin⁡θr = 6 \sin \thetar=6sinθ.

Using the same process and relationships, we can also convert polar equations to rectangular equations.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

Rewrite the polar equation r=2sin⁡θr = 2 \sin \thetar=2sinθ in Cartesian form.

The equation uses sine which means we can directly substitute using y=rsin⁡θy = r \sin \thetay=rsinθ. After that we will just need to substitute for rrr so that the entire equation is in terms of xxx and yyy...

r=2sin⁡θr=2(yr)r2=2(yr)rr2=2yx2+y2=2yx2+y2−2y=0x2+y2−2y+1−1=0x2+(y−1)2−1=0x2+(y−1)2=1\begin{array}{cccccccccccccccccccc} r &=& 2 \sin \theta \\[1em] r &=& 2 \left(\dfrac{y}{r}\right) \\[1em] r^2 &=& 2 \left(\dfrac{y}{r}\right) r \\[1em] r^2 &=& 2y \\[1em] x^2 + y^2 &=& 2y \\[1em] x^2 + y^2 - 2y &=& 0 \\[1em] x^2 + y^2 - 2y + 1 - 1 &=& 0 \\[1em] x^2 + (y - 1)^2 - 1 &=& 0 \\[1em] x^2 + (y - 1)^2 &=& 1 \\[1em] \end{array}rrr2r2x2+y2x2+y2−2yx2+y2−2y+1−1x2+(y−1)2−1x2+(y−1)2​=========​2sinθ2(ry​)2(ry​)r2y2y0001​

Therefore, the Cartesian form of the equation is x2+(y−1)2=1x^2 + (y - 1)^2 = 1x2+(y−1)2=1. Note that this is the equation of a circle with center at (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1) and radius 111.
