Angles

Angles are a fundemental concept in mathematics that appear in engineering, science, and everyday life. It is important to define them and understand their properties. By doing so, we will have a powerful tool to solve problems and understand the world around us.

Defining Angles​

Before we can define an angle, we must first define a ray. A ray is a directed line segment. It consists of one point on the line referred to as the endpoint and all points extending in one direction from that point. Finally, We can reference a ray by its endpoint and any other point on the ray. For example, we can refer to the ray with endpoint AAA and passing through point BBB as AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB.

note

The rays AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB and BA→\overrightarrow{BA}BA are different because they have two different endpoints and thus point in different directions. The first letter in the notation of a ray is always the endpoint.

Now that we have defined a ray, we can define an angle. An angle is the union of two rays that share a common endpoint which is called the vertex of the angle. Angles are named using a point on each ray and the vertex. For example, the angle formed by rays AB→\overrightarrow{AB}AB and AC→\overrightarrow{AC}AC is denoted as ∠BAC\angle BAC∠BAC where the vertex is AAA.

When creating an angle, we typically start with two rays lying on top of each other. We leave one ray fixed and this is called the initial side of the angle. We then rotate the other ray which is called the terminal side in order to create the angle.

The amount we rotate the terminal side from the initial side is called the measure of an angle. There are various units to measure angles and one of the most common units is degrees. One degree is defined as 1360\frac{1}{360}3601​ of a full circular rotation. This means a complete circular rotation contains 360360360 degrees. Finally, to denote an angle in degrees, we use the symbol °\degree°. For example, an angle of 45°45\degree45° means we have rotated the terminal side 454545 degrees from the initial side.

note

Greek letters are often used to denote angles. The most common letters used are θ\thetaθ (theta), ϕ\phiϕ (phi), α\alphaα (alpha), β\betaβ (beta), and γ\gammaγ (gamma).

Coordinate Plane​

To formalize drawing angles, we can use the xxx-yyy coordinate plane. We can draw angles anywhere on the plane but to be able to properly compare between angles, we need to define a standard position. An angle is in standard position if its vertex is located at the origin, and its initial side extends along the positive xxx-axis.

If the angle is measured in the counterclockwise direction from the initial side to the terminal side, then the angle is positive. On the other hand, if the angle is measured in the clockwise direction from the initial side to the terminal side, then the angle is negative.

Given an angle measure in degrees, we can draw the angle in standard position by...

  1. Expressing the angle measure as a fraction of 360°360\degree360°.
  2. Reducing the fraction to simplest form.
  3. Drawing an angle that contains the same fraction of the circle, beginning on the positive xxx-axis and rotating counterclockwise if the angle is positive or clockwise if the angle is negative.

note

Due to the fact that we define angles in standard position, there are a special type of angles that exist called the quadrantal angles. These angles are formed when the terminal side of the angle lies on one of the axes. The quadrantal angles are 0°0\degree0°, 90°90\degree90°, 180°180\degree180°, 270°270\degree270°, and 360°360\degree360°.

Radians​

Dividing a circle into 360360360 is arbitrary choice that is used for degrees. It is convenient as it is divisible by many numbers but it is not the only way to measure angles.

We can also measure angles less arbitarily by using radians. Before we can define radians, we must first define an arc which may be a portion of a full circle, a full circle, or more than a full circle, represented by more than one full rotation. An arc length is the length of an arc and the arc length of a full circle is called the circumference.

The circumference of a circle is C=2πrC = 2\pi rC=2πr where rrr is the radius of the circle. If we divide both sides by rrr, we get the ratio of the circumference to the radius which is 2π2\pi2π. This means that regardless of the size of the circle, the ratio of the circumference to the radius is always 2π2\pi2π. The unit radians uses the 2π2\pi2π as a reference point to measure angles.

Arc Length​

As stated before, arc length is the length of an arc. Just as the full circumference of a circle always has a constant ratio to the radius, the arc length produced by any given angle also has a constant relation to the radius, regardless of the length of the radius. This ratio is called the radian measure and it allows us to define arc length sss as s=rθs = r\thetas=rθ or θ=sr\theta = \frac{s}{r}θ=rs​ where θ\thetaθ is the angle in radians and rrr is the radius.

If we let the arc length sss be equal to rrr, then the angle θ\thetaθ is equal to 111 radian. This means that an angle of 111 radian is the angle that produces an arc length equal to the radius of the circle. With this property, we can define radians as the angle that produces an arc length equal to the radius of the circle.

note

A full circle is 2π2\pi2π radians or 360°360\degree360°. This means a half circle is π\piπ radians or 180°180\degree180° and a quarter circle is π2\frac{\pi}{2}2π​ radians or 90°90\degree90°. Finally, a single radian is equal to 180π°\frac{180}{\pi}\degreeπ180​° which is approximately 57.3°57.3\degree57.3°.

Special Angles​

Due to the fact that radian measure is the ratio of two lengths, it is a unitless measure as the units cancel out. For example, if the unit is in meters, then the arc length is in meters and the radius is in meters which means when we divide the two, the meters cancel out. This makes radians a powerful tool to measure angles as it is independent of the units used.

Coterminal Angles​

Working with angles positive or negative outside the range of 0°0\degree0° to 360°360\degree360° or 000 to 2π2\pi2π can be cumbersome. It would be easier if we were to replace out-of-range angles with a corresponding angle within the range of a single revolution.

We are essentially finding the angle that is coterminal with the given angle. Coterminal angles are two angles in standard position that have the same terminal side. For example, the angles 30°30\degree30° and 390°390\degree390° are coterminal because when we draw them in standard position, they have the same terminal side.

note

Any angle has infinetely many coterminal angles because each time we add or subtract a full revolution of 360°360\degree360° or 2π2\pi2π radians, we get a new coterminal angle.

Given an angle θ\thetaθ, we can find a coterminal angle between 0°0\degree0° and 360°360\degree360° using the following steps...

  1. Verify if θ\thetaθ is not between 0°0\degree0° and 360°360\degree360°. If it is, then we are done.
  2. Subtract 360°360\degree360° from θ\thetaθ if θ\thetaθ is greater than 360°360\degree360°.
  3. Add 360°360\degree360° to θ\thetaθ if θ\thetaθ is less than 0°0\degree0°.
  4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 until θ\thetaθ is between 0°0\degree0° and 360°360\degree360°.
  5. The final angle is the coterminal angle.

We can use the same steps to find a coterminal angle between 000 and 2π2\pi2π radians. We just need to replace 0°0\degree0° with 000 and 360°360\degree360° with 2π2\pi2π.

Example‾\underline{Example}Example​

Find an angle of measure θ\thetaθ that is coterminal with an angle of measure −17π6-\frac{17\pi}{6}−617π​.

The angle −17π6-\frac{17\pi}{6}−617π​ is not between 000 and 2π2\pi2π so we need to find a coterminal angle. We also can note that −17π6-\frac{17\pi}{6}−617π​ is less than 000 so we need to add 2π2\pi2π to the angle until it is between 000 and 2π2\pi2π...

−17π6=−17π6+2π−17π6=−5π6+2π−17π6=7π6\begin{array}{lllll} -\dfrac{17\pi}{6} = -\dfrac{17\pi}{6} + 2\pi \\[1em] \phantom{-\dfrac{17\pi}{6}} = -\dfrac{5\pi}{6} + 2\pi \\[1em] \phantom{-\dfrac{17\pi}{6}} = \dfrac{7\pi}{6} \\[1em] \end{array}−617π​=−617π​+2π−617π​=−65π​+2π−617π​=67π​​

The angle −17π6-\frac{17\pi}{6}−617π​ is coterminal with 7π6\frac{7\pi}{6}67π​.

Area of a Sector​

In addition to arc length, we can also use angles to find the area of a sector of a circle. A sector is a region of a circle bounded by two radii and the intercepted arc. Recall that to find the area of a circle, we use the formula A=πr2A = \pi r^2A=πr2 where rrr is the radius of the circle. A sector is a part of a circle so if the two radii form an angle θ\thetaθ, then θ2π\frac{\theta}{2\pi}2πθ​ is the ratio of the angle measure to the measure of a full rotation. The area of a sector is the fraction θ2π\frac{\theta}{2\pi}2πθ​ multiplied by the area of the circle where rrr is the radius of the circle and θ\thetaθ is the angle in radians. Linear and Angular Speed​

Other than using angles to find the area of a sector, we can use angles to describe the speed of a moving object. The linear speed is speed along a straight path and can be determined by the distance it moves along the path in a given time interval. In mathematical terms, the linear speed is v=stv = \dfrac{s}{t}v=ts​ where vvv is the linear speed, sss is displacement, and ttt is time.

In contrast, the angular speed is the speed resulting from an object moving along a circular path. This speed can be determined by the angle through which a point rotates in a given time interval. In mathematical terms, the angular speed is ω=θt\omega = \dfrac{\theta}{t}ω=tθ​ where ω\omegaω is the angular speed, θ\thetaθ is the angle, and ttt is time.

We can combine the definition of angular speed with the arc length equation, s=rθs = r\thetas=rθ, to find a relationship between angular and linear speeds. We can take the angular speed equation, ω=θt\omega = \dfrac{\theta}{t}ω=tθ​ and solve for θ\thetaθ to get θ=ωt\theta = \omega tθ=ωt. We can then substitute for θ\thetaθ in the arc length equation to get s=rωts = r\omega ts=rωt. Finally, we can substitute for sss in the linear speed equation, v=stv = \dfrac{s}{t}v=ts​, to get v=rωtt=rωv = \dfrac{r\omega t}{t} = r \omegav=trωt​=rω.

This means that the linear speed of an object moving along a circular path is equal to the radius of the circle multiplied by the angular speed of an object. The formula for the linear speed of an object moving along a circular path is v=rωv = r \omegav=rω where vvv is the linear speed, rrr is the radius of the circle, and ω\omegaω is the angular speed.
