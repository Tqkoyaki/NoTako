Number Theory

Number theory is a part of mathematics that examines the abstract properties of numbers.

Division Theorem​

Division is one of the four basic mathematical operations which allows us to split numbers into equal amounts. The theorem that defines how division works is the division theorem. This theorem states: Let aaa, bbb be integers, b>0b > 0b>0. Then there are unique integers qqq, rrr such that a=q⋅b+ra = q \cdot b + ra=q⋅b+r and 0≤r<b0 \leq r < b0≤r<b.

General Theorem​

The issue with the division theorem we stated above is that it only covers the cases where b>0b > 0b>0. The General Division Theorem states: Let aaa, bbb be integers, b≠0b \neq 0b=0. Then there are unique integers qqq, rrr such that a=qb+ra = qb + ra=qb+r and 0≤r≤∣b∣0 \leq r \leq |b|0≤r≤∣b∣.

Theorem: The General Division Theorem

Proof: We have proved the result in the case b>0b > 0b>0, so assume b<0b < 0b<0.

Then, since ∣b∣>0|b| > 0∣b∣>0, the previous theorem tells us there are unique integers q′q^{\prime}q′, r′r^{\prime}r′ such that a=q′⋅∣b∣+r′a = q^{\prime} \cdot |b| + r^{\prime}a=q′⋅∣b∣+r′ and 0≤r′<∣b∣0 \leq r^{\prime} < |b|0≤r′<∣b∣.

Let q=−q′q = -q^{\prime}q=−q′, r=r′r = r^{\prime}r=r′. Then, since ∣b∣=−b|b| = -b∣b∣=−b, we got a=qb+ra = qb + ra=qb+r, 0≤r≤∣b∣0 \leq r \leq |b|0≤r≤∣b∣.

note

In the division theorem, The variable qqq refers to the quotient of aaa by bbb, and rrr is the remainder.

Also note this theorem does not work for b=0b = 0b=0 because we cannot divide by zero.

Divisibility​

If division of aaa by bbb produces a remainder r=0r = 0r=0, we say aaa is divisible by bbb. Hence, aaa is divisible by bbb iff there is an integer qqq such that a=b⋅qa = b \cdot qa=b⋅q. For example, 454545 is divisible by 999 because 45=9⋅545 = 9 \cdot 545=9⋅5 but 444444 is not divisible by 999 because 44=9⋅4+844 = 9 \cdot 4 + 844=9⋅4+8.

We denote divisibility by b∣ab|ab∣a which means aaa is divisible by bbb. b∣ab|ab∣a is not the same as b/ab/ab/a because b∣ab|ab∣a refers to the relationship between aaa and bbb which is either true or false. On the other hand b/ab/ab/a denotes a rational number which is the result of dividing bbb by aaa.

Fundemental Theorem of Arithmetic​

Finally, the Fundemental Theorem of Arithmetic states that every natural number greater than 111 is either prime or can be expressed as a product of primes in a way that is unique except for the order in which they are written. The expression of a number as a product of primes is called its prime decomposition.

note

In order to proof uniqueness for this theorem, we need Euclid's Lemma which states If a prime ppp divides a product ababab, then ppp divides at least one of aaa, bbb.

Theorem: The Fundemental Theorem of Arithmetic

Proof: In order to prove this theorem, we will prove existence first, then uniqueness.

We will prove existence using proof by contradiction.

Suppose there were a composite number (i.e. non prime) that could not be written as a product of primes. Then there must be a smallest such number. Call it nnn. Since nnn is not prime, there are numbers aaa, bbb with 1<a,b<n1 < a, b < n1<a,b<n such that n=abn = abn=ab.

If aaa, bbb are primes, then n=abn = abn=ab is a prime decomposition of nnn and we have a contradiction.

If either of aaa, bbb is composite, then because it is less than nnn, it must be a product of primes, so by replacing one or both aaa, bbb by its prime decomposition in n=abn = abn=ab, we get a prime decomposition of nnn, and again we have a contradiction. That proves existence.

Now we can prove uniqueness. The prime decomposition of any natural number n>1n > 1n>1 is unique not including the order the primes. We can prove this by contradiction.

Assume there is a number n>1n > 1n>1 that has two (or more) different prime decompositions. Let nnn be the smallest such number.

Let n=p1,p2,...,pr=q1,q2,...,qsn = p_1, p_2, ..., p_r = q_1, q_2, ..., q_sn=p1​,p2​,...,pr​=q1​,q2​,...,qs​ be two different prime decompositions of nnn.
